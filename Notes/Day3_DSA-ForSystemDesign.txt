
#  22 – Graph Basics

==> Graph

A graph is a non-linear data structure where nodes (vertices) are connected using edges. Unlike trees, graphs can have multiple connections and cycles.

Why it matters:
Graphs are everywhere in real systems like social networks, maps, and recommendation engines. Understanding graphs is the foundation of many system design problems.

---

==> Nodes and Edges

Nodes represent entities, and edges represent relationships between them.

Why it matters:
Every real-world system (users, services, locations) can be modeled as nodes and their interactions as edges.

---

==> Types of Graphs

-> Undirected Graph

Connections go both ways, meaning if A is connected to B, B is also connected to A.

Why it matters:
Used in systems where relationships are mutual, like friendships or network connectivity.

---

-> Directed Graph

Connections have direction, meaning A → B does not imply B → A.

Why it matters:
Important for modeling flows like APIs, task dependencies, or follower systems.

---

-> Cyclic Graph

A graph that contains a cycle, meaning you can come back to the same node.

Why it matters:
Helps in detecting loops in systems like deadlocks, dependency cycles, or routing paths.

---

-> Weighted Graph

Edges have weights (cost, distance, time, etc.).

Why it matters:
Used in real-world systems like maps, pricing systems, and network latency calculations.

---

==> Graph Representation

-> Adjacency Matrix

A 2D matrix where 1 represents a connection and 0 means no connection.

Why it matters:
Fast to check if two nodes are connected, but consumes more space.

---

-> Adjacency List

Each node stores a list of its connected nodes.

Why it matters:
Memory efficient and commonly used in real systems.

---

#  23 – Graph Representation in Detail

==> Adjacency Matrix Explanation

Each row and column represent nodes, and values indicate whether a connection exists.

Why it matters:
Useful when the graph is dense and frequent lookups are required.

---

==> Adjacency List Explanation

Each node maintains a list of its neighbors.

Why it matters:
Preferred in most applications because it scales better with large graphs.

---

==> Sparse vs Dense Graph

Sparse graphs have fewer edges, dense graphs have many edges.

Why it matters:
Helps decide whether to use adjacency list or matrix in system design.

---

==> Space Complexity Insight

Adjacency matrix uses O(V²), adjacency list uses O(V + E).

Why it matters:
Choosing the wrong representation can lead to performance issues in large-scale systems.

---

#  24 – Graph Traversal

==> DFS (Depth First Search)

Traverse deep into the graph before backtracking. Usually implemented using recursion or stack.

Why it matters:
Used in problems like cycle detection, path finding, and dependency resolution.

---

==> Visited Array

Tracks which nodes are already visited to avoid infinite loops.

Why it matters:
Essential for correctness in graphs with cycles.

---

==> BFS (Breadth First Search)

Traverse level by level using a queue.

Why it matters:
Used for shortest path in unweighted graphs and level-wise processing.

---

==> Queue in BFS

Nodes are processed in FIFO order.

Why it matters:
Ensures correct traversal order for shortest path problems.

---

==> Difference Between DFS and BFS

DFS goes deep, BFS explores neighbors first.

Why it matters:
Choosing the right traversal impacts performance and correctness in real systems.

---

#  25 – Shortest Path & Advanced Concepts

==> Dijkstra’s Algorithm

Finds shortest path from a source node in weighted graphs without negative weights.

Why it matters:
Used in maps, routing systems, and network optimization.

---

==> Bellman-Ford Algorithm

Handles graphs with negative weights.

Why it matters:
Useful in financial systems or scenarios where costs can decrease.

---

==> Floyd-Warshall Algorithm

Finds shortest paths between all pairs of nodes.

Why it matters:
Used in systems requiring global path optimization.

---

==> Articulation Points

Nodes whose removal disconnects the graph.

Why it matters:
Helps identify critical points in networks like servers or routers.

---

==> Bridges in Graph

Edges whose removal disconnects the graph.

Why it matters:
Used to detect weak links in infrastructure systems.

---

==> Prim’s Algorithm

Used to find Minimum Spanning Tree.

Why it matters:
Helps in designing cost-efficient networks.

---

==> Tree vs Graph

A tree is a special type of graph with no cycles and a hierarchical structure.

Why it matters:
Clarifies when to use tree-based vs graph-based approaches.

---

==> Connected Components

Groups of nodes where each node is reachable.

Why it matters:
Used in clustering, network segmentation, and distributed systems.

#  26 – What is System Design

===> What is System Design

System Design is the process of designing a system’s architecture, components, modules, interfaces, and data flow to meet specific requirements.

Why it matters:
This is the foundation of building real-world applications. Without proper design, systems break when scale or complexity increases.

---

===> Architecture

Architecture defines the overall structure of the system and how different parts are organized.

Why it matters:
A good architecture ensures the system is scalable, maintainable, and easy to extend in the future.

---

===> Components

Components are individual parts of the system like services, databases, APIs, etc.

Why it matters:
Breaking a system into components makes it easier to build, test, and scale independently.

---

===> Modules

Modules are smaller units inside components that handle specific functionality.

Why it matters:
Helps in writing clean, reusable, and maintainable code.

---

===> Interfaces (Interaction)

Interfaces define how different components communicate with each other.

Why it matters:
Clear interfaces prevent tight coupling and make systems easier to modify or replace.

---

===> Data Flow

Data flow defines how data moves between components in the system.

Why it matters:
Efficient data flow ensures performance and avoids bottlenecks.

---

===> Designing

Designing means mapping requirements into system components and structuring them properly.

Why it matters:
This is where real engineering decisions happen, not just coding.

---

===> Why System Design – Scalability

Scalability means the system can handle increasing users or traffic without breaking.

Why it matters:
Real systems grow over time, and without scalability, they fail under load.

---

===> Traffic Growth Understanding

Systems may grow from 100 users to thousands or even millions.

Why it matters:
You must design systems that work both at small and large scale without rewriting everything.

---

#  27 – Key System Design Principles

===> Performance

Performance is about how fast the system responds under load.

Why it matters:
Users expect fast responses; slow systems lead to poor experience and drop-offs.

---

===> Dependency Handling (Payment Example)

If a third-party system like a bank fails, your system should still handle requests properly.

Why it matters:
Real systems depend on external services, so you must design for failures.

---

===> Reliability

Reliability means the system should work correctly even during traffic spikes or failures.

Why it matters:
Users trust systems that are always available and consistent.

---

===> Maintainability

Maintainability means the system should be easy to update and modify.

Why it matters:
Systems evolve over time, and difficult-to-maintain systems slow down development.

---

===> Cost Efficiency

System should balance performance and cost, avoiding unnecessary expenses.

Why it matters:
Over-engineering wastes money, and under-engineering causes failures.

---

===> Fault Tolerance

System should continue working even if some components fail.

Why it matters:
Failures are inevitable, so systems must handle them gracefully.

---

===> Load Handling

System should distribute load properly across resources.

Why it matters:
Prevents overload on a single component and improves performance.

---

#  29 – How System Design Works (Food Delivery Example)

===> Thinking Approach

You should think how the system is designed, how it works, and why decisions are made.

Why it matters:
System Design is about reasoning, not memorizing.

---

===> Requirement Gathering

Clearly define what the system should do.

Why it matters:
Wrong requirements lead to wrong design.

---

===> Must-have vs Good-to-have

Separate core features from optional ones.

Why it matters:
Helps prioritize and build systems incrementally.

---

===> Core Features (Food Delivery App)

-> Real-time Restaurant Listing

Shows available restaurants dynamically.

Why it matters:
Ensures users see correct and updated data.

---

-> Payment Integration

Supports UPI, cards, COD, etc.

Why it matters:
Flexible payments improve conversion.

---

-> Offers & Categorization

Filters and organizes items.

Why it matters:
Improves discoverability and user experience.

---

-> Real-time Order Tracking

Track order status live.

Why it matters:
Builds trust and transparency.

---

===> Search Functionality

Search based on rating, price, location, food type.

Why it matters:
Efficient search improves usability.

---

===> User Flow Understanding

User selects items → places order → pays → tracks delivery.

Why it matters:
Helps design APIs and data flow correctly.

---

===> Real-time Updates

Continuous updates for order and delivery.

Why it matters:
Requires event-driven or real-time systems.

---

===> Component-based Design

Break system into smaller components instead of writing everything together.

Why it matters:
Makes system scalable, maintainable, and easier to build.

---

===> Independent Component Design

Each component should be designed separately.

Why it matters:
Enables independent scaling and development.
